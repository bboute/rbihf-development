Introduction 
Belgian ice hockey needs a clear, age-appropriate and long-term youth development pathway. 
If we want to grow into icehockey and become more competitive internationally, we must 
start by improving the way young players are developed inside our clubs. 
At this moment, Belgian ice hockey relies on motivated clubs, committed volunteers and 
passionate coaches. However, the development structure is not always consistent from club to 
club. Some clubs have a clear vision and training structure, while others mainly operate in a 
recreational environment with limited guidance on how to develop players step by step. This 
creates differences in player development and makes it difficult to build a strong national 
pathway. 
Belgium is a small hockey country, with a limited number of clubs, players and ice rinks. 
Because of that, every club plays an important role in the future of Belgian ice hockey. We 
cannot afford to have player development depend only on a few clubs or a few individual 
coaches. Every Belgian club needs to contribute to the development of better hockey players. 
In recent years, we have seen that Belgium is struggling to consistently compete and hold its 
position on international level. This is not only a national team issue. National team results 
are the outcome of the development work that happens many years earlier in the clubs. If we 
want stronger U18, U20 and senior national teams in the future, we need better-developed 
players entering the pathway at a younger age. 
The foundation of player development must therefore be built at U8, U10 and U12 level. 
These are the most important foundation years. At these ages, the focus should not be on 
systems, results or early specialization, but on skating, puck skills, movement, confidence, 
decision-making, fun, creativity and love for the game. 
This development plan is created to give Belgian clubs, coaches and national programs a 
shared direction. It does not replace the work of the clubs. Instead, it provides a common 
framework that helps every club understand what players should learn at each age, how 
practices should be organized, and how we can create a stronger connection between club 
development, Talent Pool and National Team level. 
The future of Belgian ice hockey depends on the quality of the foundation we build today.

Purpose 
• Establish a clear, age-appropriate development pathway for youth ice hockey in Belgium; 
• provide clubs and coaches with shared development principles and a common coaching 
direction; 
• Define the key skills, habits and learning objectives for each age category; 
• Describe how practices should be structured to match the needs of players at each 
development stage; 
• Prevent early over-coaching, tactical overload, early specialization and result-driven 
development at young ages; 
• Strengthen the connection between club development, the Talent Pool program and the 
Belgian National Teams. 
The plan does not replace the work of club coaches. Instead, it provides a shared framework 
that helps every club develop players in a more consistent and age-appropriate way. 
Philosophy 
Players must first learn to skate, move, play, think and enjoy the game before they are 
expected to perform within complex systems. 
At younger ages, development should focus on fundamental skills, confidence, creativity and 
enjoyment. Players need many repetitions, many puck touches and many game-like 
situations. They should be active, involved and challenged in a positive environment.

Development Phases 
 
The Belgian Youth Development is built around three progressive development phases: 
Learn to Play, Play to Compete and Play to Win. These phases describe how the focus of 
player development should evolve as players grow older and move closer to national team 
and senior hockey environments. 
 
The model should not be seen as three completely separate blocks. Each phase builds on the 
previous one. Players cannot successfully compete if they have not first developed a strong 
technical foundation. In the same way, players cannot be expected to perform in a “Play to 
Win” environment if they have not learned how to compete with structure, intensity and 
responsibility. 
The largest part of the pyramid is Learn to Play, because this is the foundation of 
development. The U8, U10 and U12 age groups are the most important years for building 
skating ability, puck skills, confidence, creativity and love for the game. If this foundation is 
weak, the higher levels of the pathway will also be limited.

The Play to Compete phase, covering U14 and U16, introduces more intensity, 
game understanding and tactical responsibility. Players learn to apply their skills 
under pressure and within a more competitive environment. Development remains the 
priority, but players are gradually prepared for higher standards. 
The Play to Win phase, starting at U18, connects development to performance. At this stage, 
players should be prepared for senior hockey, national team expectations and international 
competition. Individual development continues, but team identity, roles, discipline, 
preparation and results become more important. 
This pathway gives Belgian clubs and coaches a shared direction: build the foundation first, 
teach players how to compete, and only then expect them to perform in a high-performance 
environment.

Learn to play: U8-U10-U12 
General information 
Station-Based Practices 
Station-based practices should be the preferred practice model in the Learn to Play phase. 
Instead of having one large group doing the same drill, players are divided into smaller 
groups and rotate between different stations. 
This allows players to receive more repetitions, more puck touches and more individual 
feedback. It also keeps the practice active and avoids long waiting lines. 
A good station-based practice can include 4 to 6 stations, depending on the number of 
players, coaches and available ice space. 
Groups 
Groups should ideally contain a maximum of 5 to 6 players per station. This keeps the 
activity level high and allows the coach to give simple, direct feedback. 
With 5 or 6 stations, a club can organize an effective practice for 30 to 36 players. At this 
moment, most Belgian clubs do not have these numbers in one age category, but the structure 
gives clubs a model that can grow with them. 
Coaching Staff and Roles 
Every practice should have at least one main coach. The main coach is responsible for the 
practice plan, the organization of the session and the key development focus of the day. 
The ideal practice setup includes: 
• 1 main coach responsible for the full practice ; 
• The main coach is responsible for station rotation and timing; 
• 1 experienced coach who moves between stations and supports the station coaches; 
This can also be the main coach. 
• several station coaches or helpers who manage the exercises.  
• support from senior players, junior players, assistant coaches or trained parents where 
needed. 
Not every club will have access to professional coaches or a large coaching staff. In that case, 
clubs can use parents and volunteers as support staff. However, these helpers must receive 
clear instructions from the main coach. They do not need to be technical experts, but they 
must understand the goal of the station, keep players active and help create a safe and 
positive learning environment.

Timekeeping 
Timekeeping is strongly recommended for station-based practices. This person manages the 
clock, signals when groups rotate and helps the practice stay organized. 
This allows the coaches to focus on coaching instead of managing time. It also gives the 
practice rhythm and prevents stations from running too long or too short. 
Small-Area Games 
Small-area games should be included in every Learn to Play practice. These games allow 
players to apply their skills in realistic situations with more puck touches, more decisions and 
more involvement than full-ice drills. 
For U8, U10 and U12 players, small-area games help develop: 
• puck protection; 
• 1v1 skills; 
• support play; 
• quick decision-making; 
• creativity; 
• competitiveness; 
• transition between attack and defence. 
• Overall skill ( skating, puckhandling... ) 
Small-area games should be used as a development tool, not only as a fun activity at the 
end of practice. 
Avoiding Long Lines and Over-Coaching 
Young players learn by doing. Practices should avoid long explanations, long waiting lines 
and too much correction. So demonstrate drills instead of talking inf ront of a whiteboard. 
Coaches should use short instructions, clear demonstrations and simple coaching points. 
Players should be allowed to make mistakes, solve problems and learn through repetition. 
The goal is not to create perfect players at U8, U10 or U12. The goal is to create 
confident, skilled and motivated players who are ready for the next stage of 
development.

1. Main objective 
The main objective at U8-U10-U12 is to create a fun, safe and positive first experience with 
ice hockey. Players should enjoy coming to the rink, feel comfortable on the ice and develop 
a lasting connection with the sport. 
At this age, enjoyment and player retention are the main measures of success. Technical 
development is important, but it should always be delivered through playful, active and age-
appropriate activities.

2. Layout  
Example Full Station Practice: 34 Players 
For a large group of 34 players, divide the players into six groups: 
• four groups of six players;  
• two groups of five players.  
The full ice is divided into six stations. Each group starts at a different station and rotates in 
the same direction when the timekeeper gives the signal. 
Station Development Focus 
Station 1 Balance and agility 
Station 2 Starting and stopping 
Station 3 Turning and edge control 
Station 4 Puck control 
Station 5 Passing or scoring game 
Station 6 Small-area game 
Players should spend approximately 8 minutes at each station. Allowing time for short 
demonstrations and rotations, the station block requires approximately 55–60 minutes. 
Each station should have one coach or instructed helper. One main coach oversees the full 
practice, keeping time and adjusting where needed. 
This format allows a club to organise an active and development-focused practice for 34 
players while maintaining groups of no more than six. 
Example Practice: 16 Players 
Divide the players into three small groups: two groups of five and one group of six. 
Begin the practice with a 10–15-minute fun warm-up game involving all players. This 
creates immediate activity, enjoyment and engagement. 
Continue with three skill stations. Each group spends approximately eight minutes at each 
station. Including short demonstrations and rotations, this block takes approximately 30 
minutes.

3. LTP Outcomes 
Throughout the Learn to Play phase, players should progressively develop the technical, 
physical, social and personal foundations needed to continue their hockey journey. 
By the end of the Learn to Play phase, players should ideally: 
Skating and Movement 
• move confidently and independently on the ice;  
• demonstrate balance, agility and control;  
• start, accelerate, stop and change direction effectively;  
• skate forward and backward with developing technique;  
• use their edges and maintain control during different movements.  
Puck Skills 
• control the puck on the forehand and backhand;  
• carry the puck while moving and changing direction;  
• pass and receive while stationary and in motion;  
• direct the puck towards the net;  
• combine skating and puck skills in game situations.  
Game Understanding 
• participate actively in small-area and age-appropriate games;  
• recognise changes between attack and defence;  
• find open space and support the puck carrier;  
• attempt to recover the puck when defending;  
• make simple decisions under pressure;  
• cooperate with teammates;  
• experience different roles and positions.  
Personal Development 
• enjoy attending practices and games;  
• feel safe, valued and included;  
• listen, learn and attempt new challenges; 
• demonstrate respect towards teammates, coaches and opponents;  
• develop confidence, creativity and independence;  
• build a lasting love for hockey.  
The most important outcome is a player who enjoys the game and wants to continue playing. 
Fun drives retention, and retention creates long-term development. 
These outcomes are development targets, not selection criteria. Children develop at different 
speeds and should not be prevented from progressing because they have not yet mastered 
every skill. The age-specific skills matrices define the expected technical progression for U8, 
U10 and U12.

4. What to avoid  
Practices and games during the Learn to Play phase should not resemble adult or 
performance-level hockey. Coaches and clubs should avoid: 
• long explanations and complicated coaching language;  
• long lines and activities with limited participation;  
• full-ice drills involving only a small number of players;  
• conditioning without a puck, game or playful purpose;  
• excessive tactical instruction and complex systems;  
• prioritising systems over individual skill development;  
• fixing players into positions too early;  
• forcing early goaltender specialisation;  
• spending excessive ice time on isolated shooting or stickhandling technique;  
• eliminating players from games or activities;  
• punishing mistakes or unsuccessful skill attempts;  
• using skating, laps or physical exercises as punishment;  
• giving stronger players more ice time or attention;  
• focusing on scores, standings and tournament results;  
• measuring development only through formal testing;  
• reducing creativity through constant correction.  
At U8 and U10, clubs should use a goaltender rotation system so every interested player can 
experience the position. At U12, players may begin showing a positional preference, but  
opportunities should remain flexible

U8 
U8 players should participate in a minimum of 15 tournaments between September and 
April/May. During each tournament, players should play at least four 15-minute cross-ice 
mini-games against teams from different clubs. 
Fifteen tournament mornings across an eight-month season represents approximately one 
event every two weeks. This creates regular game exposure without allowing competition to 
replace practice and skill development. 
U10 
U10 players should participate in a minimum of 20 tournaments during the season. Each 
event should provide at least four 15-minute cross-ice mini-games. 
The higher number of events gives U10 players more opportunities to apply their developing 
skating, puck skills and decision-making in game situations. 
U12 
U12 players should participate in a minimum of 30 games during the season: 
• September-December: cross-ice competition;  
• January-April/May: progression to full-ice competition.  
The cross-ice period maximises puck touches, involvement and decision-making. Full-ice 
competition is introduced during the second half of the season so players can gradually learn 
spacing, positioning and basic team principles.

6. Game philosophy 
Games are an important part of player development. They allow children to apply their skills, 
make decisions, compete and experience the enjoyment of playing hockey. During the Learn 
to Play phase, games should support development rather than focus primarily on results. 
Meaningful playing time 
Every player should receive meaningful and approximately equal playing time. Participation 
should not depend on current ability, physical maturity or the score of the game. 
All players need opportunities to: 
• play in different situations;  
• handle the puck;  
• make decisions;  
• experience success and failure;  
• contribute to their team.  
No shortening the bench 
Coaches should not reduce the number of players being used to win a game. Every selected 
player should remain involved throughout the event. 
Players must not lose playing time because they make mistakes. Mistakes are a necessary part 
of development and should be used as learning opportunities. 
Position rotation 
U8 and U10 players should rotate through all skater positions. Clubs should also provide 
interested players with opportunities to experience goaltending. 
RBIHF can help with interchangeable goalie gear! 
At U12, players may begin to develop positional preferences. However, continued rotation is 
encouraged to develop broader game understanding and prevent premature specialisation. 
Development before results 
Coaching decisions should be based on long-term player development rather than short-term 
results. The quality of play, individual improvement, effort, creativity and decision-making 
are more important than the final score.

Play to Compete: U14-U16 
General information 
 
The Play to Compete phase includes the U14 and U16 age categories. This phase is the 
bridge between the Learn to Play foundation and the Play to Win environment at U18. 
 
At this stage, players should already have a basic technical foundation. The focus now shifts 
toward applying those skills at higher speed, under pressure and within more realistic game 
situations. Players must learn how to compete, make decisions, support teammates, defend 
with purpose and understand basic team principles. 
 
Development remains the priority. However, the environment should become more 
demanding. Players should be challenged to train with intensity, accept feedback, compete 
consistently and take more responsibility for their own development. 
 
The Play to Compete phase should not become only about winning games. Winning becomes 
part of the learning environment, but the main goal is to prepare players for the standards 
required at U18, Talent Pool and National Team level. 
 
Station-Based Practices 
 
Station-based practices can still be used in the Play to Compete phase, but they should no 
longer be the main practice model all the time. 
 
 U14 and U16, stations are useful for: 
 
- individual skill development; 
- skating and edge work; 
- shooting repetitions; 
- puck protection; 
- body contact preparation; 
- position-specific work; 
- small-group tactical situations. 
 
However, players at this age also need more connected drills and game-like situations. 
Practices should include flow drills, transition drills, 1v1, 2v1, 2v2, 3v2 situations, small-area 
games and basic team concepts.

## Groups 
 
Group size can be slightly larger than in Learn to Play, depending on the drill objective. For 
technical work, smaller groups are still preferred because they create more repetitions and 
better feedback. 
 
For tactical and game-like drills, groups may be larger because players must learn timing, 
spacing, support, transition and role responsibility. 
 
As a general guideline: 
 
| Practice Type | Recommended Group Size | 
|---|---:| 
| Technical station work | 5-8 players | 
| Small-area games | 6-10 players | 
| Tactical/game-situation drills | 8-12 players | 
| Team concept work | Full line or team units | 
 
The key principle remains the same: players should be active, involved and challenged. 
Practices should avoid long waiting lines and drills where only one or two players are active. 
 
 
General Practice Methodology 
 
U14 and U16 practices should be more game-like and more demanding than Learn to Play 
practices. While technical skill development remains important, players must now learn to 
execute skills under pressure, at speed and with tactical purpose. 
 
Practices should include a combination of: 
 
- skating and puck skill maintenance; 
- individual tactics; 
- 1v1, 2v1, 2v2 and 3v2 situations; 
- small-area games with specific objectives; 
- flow drills that connect skills to game situations; 
- transition drills; 
- basic team principles; 
- compete drills; 
- position-specific learning; 
- off-ice physical development. 
 
Station-based practices can still be used, especially for technical work or when large groups 
are on the ice. However, U14 and U16 players also need more connected drills where they 
learn timing, spacing, support, pressure, transition and team habits.

## Coaching Staff and Roles 
 
Every U14 and U16 practice should have one main coach responsible for the full session. The 
main coach prepares the practice plan, defines the focus points and ensures that the practice 
matches the development goals of the age group. 
 
The ideal coaching setup includes: 
 
- one main coach responsible for the full practice; 
- assistant coaches responsible for specific drills or player groups; 
- position-specific support where possible; 
- a goalie coach where available; 
- support from senior players or experienced coaches when needed. 
 
At this stage, coaches must give more detailed feedback than in Learn to Play. Players should 
be corrected on technical details, decision-making, body position, support, defensive habits 
and tactical understanding. 
 
However, coaching should still be clear and efficient. Long explanations should be avoided. 
Players learn best when coaching points are short, specific and connected to the drill or game 
situation. 
 
## Timekeeping 
 
Timekeeping remains important, but the structure is different from Learn to Play. 
 
In U14 and U16 practices, time should be managed around practice blocks rather than only 
station rotations. A typical practice may include: 
 
- warm-up and skating activation; 
- technical skill block; 
- individual or group tactical block; 
- game-situation drills; 
- small-area games or compete drills; 
- team concept work. 
 
The main coach should control the timing of each block and make sure the practice keeps 
intensity and rhythm. Drills should not run too long if the quality drops. It is better to have 
shorter, high-intensity blocks than long drills with declining focus.

## Small-Area Games 
 
Small-area games remain important in the Play to Compete phase, but they should now have 
clearer tactical objectives. 
 
At U14 and U16, small-area games should be used to develop: 
 
- puck protection under pressure; 
- support around the puck; 
- quick offensive and defensive transition; 
- 1v1 compete habits; 
- defensive-side positioning; 
- communication; 
- scanning before receiving the puck; 
- decision-making under pressure; 
- battle level and compete habits. 
 
Small-area games should not only be used as fun games at the end of practice. They should 
be planned as development tools with a specific theme, such as support below the puck, quick 
transition, net-front play, wall battles or defensive positioning. 
 
 
## Avoiding Long Lines and Over-Coaching 
 
U14 and U16 players need more structure than younger players, but practices should still 
avoid long lines, passive waiting time and excessive talking. 
 
Coaches should avoid: 
 
- drills where players wait too long between repetitions; 
- long tactical explanations before players understand the situation; 
- stopping drills too often; 
- over-coaching every mistake; 
- focusing only on systems and removing creativity; 
- using conditioning as punishment; 
- allowing only the strongest players to receive meaningful feedback; 
- turning every practice into a team systems session. 
 
Players at this age need correction, but they also need repetition, challenge and responsibility. 
Coaches should give clear feedback, allow players to try again and connect corrections to real 
game situations. 
 
The goal is to create players who can compete with skill, intelligence, discipline and 
confidence.

Play to win: U18 
 
## General Information 
 
The Play to Win phase starts at U18. This phase prepares players for senior hockey, national 
team standards and international competition. 
 
At U18, development continues, but the environment becomes more performance-driven. 
Players are expected to train with intensity, understand team structure, accept roles, prepare 
properly and compete to win. 
 
The focus is no longer only on learning basic skills or learning how to compete. Players must 
now apply their skills within a team identity and a structured game model. Individual 
development remains important, but it must support team performance. 
 
The Play to Win phase should create players who are technically capable, tactically 
responsible, physically prepared, mentally disciplined and ready for higher-level hockey. 
 
## Station-Based Practices 
 
Station-based practices are no longer the main practice model at U18, but they can still be 
used for specific purposes. 
 
At U18, stations are useful for: 
 
- position-specific skill development; 
- shooting and scoring repetitions; 
- face-off work; 
- puck protection and battle skills; 
- defensive details; 
- goaltender-specific situations; 
- special teams details; 
- individual correction. 
 
Most U18 practices should be built around connected, game-like training. Players need drills 
that include timing, pressure, reads, support, transition, team structure and execution. 
 
The focus should move from isolated repetition toward applying skills within the team game 
model.

## Groups 
 
Group organisation at U18 depends on the objective of the practice block. 
 
Smaller groups are still useful for technical detail and position-specific work. Larger groups 
and full-team units are needed for systems, special teams, breakouts, forechecks, defensive-
zone coverage and game preparation. 
 
As a general guideline: 
 
| Practice Type | Recommended Group Size | 
|---|---:| 
| Position-specific skill work | 4-8 players | 
| Battle and compete drills | 6-10 players | 
| Line or unit work | 5-10 players | 
| Team structure drills | Full team or full units | 
| Special teams | PP / PK units | 
 
The key principle at U18 is quality and purpose. Every drill should connect to individual 
improvement, team identity or game performance. 
 
## Coaching Staff and Roles 
 
Every U18 practice should have a clear coaching structure. The head coach is responsible for 
the full practice plan, team identity and performance objectives. 
 
The ideal coaching setup includes: 
 
- one head coach responsible for the full practice; 
- assistant coaches responsible for specific units or tactical areas; 
- a goalie coach where possible; 
- support for video, physical preparation and recovery where available; 
- clear communication between club coaches, Talent Pool staff and national team staff. 
 
At this stage, coaches should provide detailed feedback on execution, habits, decision-
making, positioning, discipline and role responsibility. 
 
Players should understand not only what they are doing, but why it matters within the team 
structure.

## Timekeeping 
 
Timekeeping at U18 should support intensity, rhythm and game preparation. 
 
Practices should be organised into clear blocks, such as: 
 
- activation and warm-up; 
- technical or position-specific work; 
- tactical execution; 
- transition and game-situation drills; 
- special teams; 
- compete drills; 
- game preparation or review. 
 
Practice blocks should be timed carefully. Drills should be long enough to create learning and 
repetition, but not so long that intensity, focus or execution quality drops. 
 
At U18, practice tempo matters. Players must learn to train with pace, urgency and attention 
to detail.

- compete level; 
- scoring habits; 
- defensive responsibility. 
 
Small-area games at U18 should be intense, competitive and connected to the team’s playing 
principles. They are not only a fun element, but a tool to develop habits that transfer directly 
to games.
